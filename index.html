Electrostatics Topics

Coordinate Systems

In electrostatics, problems are often solved using different coordinate systems depending on the symmetry of the problem. The three most common coordinate systems are:

  • Cartesian (Rectangular) Coordinates: \((x, y, z)\)
  • Cylindrical Coordinates: \((\rho, \phi, z)\)
  • Spherical Coordinates: \((r, \theta, \phi)\)

Each system has its own set of unit vectors and differential elements (length, area, volume) that are used in calculations.

Del Operator (\(\nabla\))

The del operator (\(\nabla\)) is a vector differential operator used in vector calculus. In Cartesian coordinates, it is defined as: \[ \nabla = \mathbf{i} \frac{\partial}{\partial x} + \mathbf{j} \frac{\partial}{\partial y} + \mathbf{k} \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \] The del operator is used to compute the gradient, divergence, and curl of scalar and vector fields.

Gradient

The gradient of a scalar field \(V\) is a vector that points in the direction of the steepest increase of \(V\). It is defined as: \[ \nabla V = \frac{\partial V}{\partial x} \mathbf{i} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial y} \mathbf{j} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial z} \mathbf{k} \] The gradient is used to calculate the electric field \(\mathbf{E}\) from the electric potential \(V\): \[ \mathbf{E} = -\nabla V \]

Divergence

The divergence of a vector field \(\mathbf{A}\) measures the net outflow of the field from an infinitesimal volume. It is defined as: \[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} = \frac{\partial A_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial A_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial A_z}{\partial z} \] In electrostatics, the divergence of the electric field \(\mathbf{E}\) is related to the charge density \(\rho\) by Gauss's law: \[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0} \]

Divergence Theorem

The divergence theorem relates the flux of a vector field through a closed surface to the volume integral of its divergence: \[ \int_V (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}) \, dV = \oint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{S} \] This theorem is used to derive Gauss's law in integral form: \[ \oint_S \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\epsilon_0} \]

Stoke’s Theorem

Stoke’s theorem relates the circulation of a vector field around a closed loop to the surface integral of its curl: \[ \oint_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \int_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{A}) \cdot d\mathbf{S} \] In electrostatics, this theorem is used to show that the curl of the electric field \(\mathbf{E}\) is zero: \[ \nabla \times \mathbf{E} = 0 \]

Introduction to Electrostatics

Electrostatics deals with the study of electric charges at rest. The fundamental law governing electrostatics is Coulomb's Law, which states that the force between two point charges \(Q_1\) and \(Q_2\) is: \[ \mathbf{F} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q_1 Q_2}{r^2} \mathbf{a}_r \] where \(\epsilon_0\) is the permittivity of free space, \(r\) is the distance between the charges, and \(\mathbf{a}_r\) is the unit vector pointing from \(Q_1\) to \(Q_2\).

Coulomb's Law

Coulomb's Law describes the force between two point charges. It states that:

The force \(\mathbf{F}\) between two point charges \(Q_1\) and \(Q_2\) is directly proportional to the product of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance \(r\) between them. The force acts along the line joining the two charges.

Mathematically, Coulomb's Law is expressed as: \[ \mathbf{F} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q_1 Q_2}{r^2} \mathbf{a}_r \]

Where:

  • \(\mathbf{F}\) is the force between the charges (in Newtons).
  • \(Q_1\) and \(Q_2\) are the magnitudes of the two charges (in Coulombs).
  • \(r\) is the distance between the charges (in meters).
  • \(\epsilon_0\) is the permittivity of free space (\(8.854 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{F/m}\)).
  • \(\mathbf{a}_r\) is the unit vector pointing from \(Q_1\) to \(Q_2\).

Derivation of Coulomb's Law

Coulomb's Law can be derived from the principles of electrostatics and the concept of the electric field.

Step 1: Electric Field Due to a Point Charge
The electric field \(\mathbf{E}\) due to a point charge \(Q\) is given by: \[ \mathbf{E} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q}{r^2} \mathbf{a}_r \]

Step 2: Force on a Charge in an Electric Field
When a charge \(Q_2\) is placed in the electric field \(\mathbf{E}\) created by \(Q_1\), the force \(\mathbf{F}\) on \(Q_2\) is: \[ \mathbf{F} = Q_2 \mathbf{E} \]

Step 3: Substituting the Electric Field
Substituting the expression for \(\mathbf{E}\) into the force equation: \[ \mathbf{F} = Q_2 \left( \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q_1}{r^2} \mathbf{a}_r \right) \] \[ \mathbf{F} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q_1 Q_2}{r^2} \mathbf{a}_r \]

This is the mathematical form of Coulomb's Law.

Electric Field Intensity

Definition

The Electric Field Intensity (\(\mathbf{E}\)) at a point in space is defined as the force experienced by a unit positive test charge (\(q_0\)) placed at that point, in the limit as \(q_0\) approaches zero. Mathematically, it is expressed as: \[ \mathbf{E} = \lim_{q_0 \to 0} \frac{\mathbf{F}}{q_0} \]

Where:

  • \(\mathbf{E}\) is the electric field intensity (in N/C or V/m).
  • \(\mathbf{F}\) is the force experienced by the test charge (in Newtons).
  • \(q_0\) is the magnitude of the test charge (in Coulombs).

Formula for Electric Field Intensity

The electric field intensity due to a point charge \(Q\) at a distance \(r\) from the charge is given by: \[ \mathbf{E} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q}{r^2} \mathbf{a}_r \]

Where:

  • \(\epsilon_0\) is the permittivity of free space (\(8.854 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{F/m}\)).
  • \(Q\) is the source charge (in Coulombs).
  • \(r\) is the distance from the charge (in meters).
  • \(\mathbf{a}_r\) is the unit vector pointing from the source charge to the point where the field is measured.

Derivation of Electric Field Intensity

The electric field intensity can be derived from Coulomb's Law.

Step 1: Coulomb's Law
According to Coulomb's Law, the force \(\mathbf{F}\) between two point charges \(Q\) and \(q_0\) is: \[ \mathbf{F} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q q_0}{r^2} \mathbf{a}_r \]

Step 2: Electric Field Intensity
The electric field intensity \(\mathbf{E}\) is defined as the force per unit charge: \[ \mathbf{E} = \frac{\mathbf{F}}{q_0} \]

Step 3: Substituting Coulomb's Law
Substituting the expression for \(\mathbf{F}\) from Coulomb's Law: \[ \mathbf{E} = \frac{1}{q_0} \left( \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q q_0}{r^2} \mathbf{a}_r \right) \] \[ \mathbf{E} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q}{r^2} \mathbf{a}_r \]

This is the expression for the electric field intensity due to a point charge.

Electric Field Due to Multiple Charges

For a system of \(n\) point charges, the total electric field at a point is the vector sum of the electric fields due to each individual charge: \[ \mathbf{E}_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{E}_i = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q_i}{r_i^2} \mathbf{a}_{r_i} \]

Electric Flux Density

Definition

Electric flux density (D) is a measure of the electric flux passing through a unit area in an electric field. It is related to the electric field intensity (E) and the permittivity of the medium.

Formula

The mathematical expression for electric flux density is given by:

D = εE

where:

  • D = Electric flux density (Coulombs per square meter, C/m²)
  • E = Electric field intensity (Volts per meter, V/m)
  • ε = Permittivity of the medium (Farads per meter, F/m)

Derivation

Starting from Gauss's law in differential form:

∇ · D = ρ

where ρ is the volume charge density (C/m³).

Using the relation D = εE, we substitute it into Gauss's law:

∇ · (εE) = ρ

If ε is constant, it can be taken out of the divergence operation:

ε (∇ · E) = ρ

Since Gauss's law in integral form states:

∮ D · dA = Q_{enc}

where Q_{enc} is the total charge enclosed, this confirms that D represents the electric flux per unit area and is fundamental in electrostatics.

Electric Flux Density for an Infinite Sheet

For an infinite sheet of charge with surface charge density σ, the electric flux density is given by:

D = σ/2

The electric field intensity E is:

E = σ / (2ε)

This result follows from applying Gauss's law to a Gaussian pillbox around the sheet.

Electric Flux Density for a Volume Charge Distribution

For a volume charge distribution with charge density ρ, the electric flux density is determined using Gauss’s law in differential form:

∇ · D = ρ

In integral form:

∮ D · dA = Q_{enc}

where Q_{enc} is the total charge enclosed within the Gaussian surface.

Calculation of Electric Field, Potential, and Energy

The electric field \(\mathbf{E}\) due to a point charge \(Q\) is given by: \[ \mathbf{E} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q}{r^2} \mathbf{a}_r \] The electric potential \(V\) at a point due to a charge distribution is: \[ V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \int \frac{dq}{r} \] The potential energy \(U\) of a system of charges is: \[ U = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i \neq j} \frac{Q_i Q_j}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r_{ij}} \]

Gauss's Law: Statement, Derivation, and Formulas

Statement of Gauss's Law

Gauss’s law states that the total electric flux ΦE through a closed surface is equal to 1/ε0 times the total charge enclosed within the surface:

∫ S E ⋅ dA = Q enc ε 0

Derivation Using the Divergence Theorem

The divergence theorem states:

∫ S F ⋅ dA = ∫ V ∇ ⋅ F dV

Applying this to Gauss’s law:

∫ S E ⋅ dA = ∫ V ∇ ⋅ E dV

Since the enclosed charge is:

Q enc = ∫ V ρ dV

We obtain the differential form of Gauss’s law:

∇ ⋅ E = ρ ε 0

Gauss's Law in Maxwell’s Equations

Integral Form

∫ S E ⋅ dA = Q ε 0

Differential Form

∇ ⋅ E = ρ ε 0

Polarization in Dielectrics

When a dielectric material is placed in an electric field, it becomes polarized. The polarization \(\mathbf{P}\) is defined as the dipole moment per unit volume: \[ \mathbf{P} = \epsilon_0 \chi_e \mathbf{E} \] where \(\chi_e\) is the electric susceptibility of the material.

Bound Charges

Polarization in a dielectric leads to the formation of bound charges. The bound surface charge density \(\sigma_b\) and bound volume charge density \(\rho_b\) are given by: \[ \sigma_b = \mathbf{P} \cdot \mathbf{n}, \quad \rho_b = -\nabla \cdot \mathbf{P} \]

Dielectric Constant and Strength

The dielectric constant \(\epsilon_r\) is a measure of how much a material can reduce the electric field: \[ \epsilon_r = 1 + \chi_e \] The dielectric strength is the maximum electric field a material can withstand without breaking down.

Continuity Equation and Relaxation Time

The continuity equation describes the conservation of charge: \[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} + \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = 0 \] The relaxation time \(\tau\) is the time it takes for a charge distribution to decay to \(1/e\) of its initial value: \[ \tau = \frac{\epsilon}{\sigma} \] where \(\sigma\) is the conductivity of the material.

Boundary Conditions

At the interface between two media, the electric field and displacement field must satisfy certain boundary conditions: \[ \mathbf{E}_{1t} = \mathbf{E}_{2t}, \quad \mathbf{D}_{1n} - \mathbf{D}_{2n} = \sigma_f \] where \(\sigma_f\) is the free surface charge density.
